Rethinking event discovery and the path to purchase

CompanyGoers
RoleProduct Designer
DurationJun 2025 – Jan 2026
IndustryEvents & Entertainment
Context

An events platform with a discovery problem

Goers is an events and experiences platform in Indonesia. Users come to browse concerts, workshops, food festivals, and activities, but the homepage wasn't surfacing the right content to the right people. The purchase journey had too many steps between "this looks interesting" and "I bought a ticket." And the event detail page was information-dense but not decision-friendly.

I joined as the sole product designer, responsible for end-to-end design and running all usability testing myself.

Challenge 1

Homepage redesign: helping users find what they didn't know they wanted

The old homepage was essentially a chronological feed. Popular events, niche workshops, and seasonal activities all competed for the same space with no personalization or curation logic. Users who came to browse (not search) had no guidance.

I redesigned the homepage around content discoverability: curated sections based on category and recency, a "trending near you" module, and improved visual hierarchy so users could scan and decide faster. The goal was to make the homepage feel like a knowledgeable friend recommending things to do this weekend.

Challenge 2

Simplifying the purchase journey

The existing purchase flow had unnecessary friction: users would find an event, tap to see details, then have to navigate through ticket selection, date selection, quantity, and payment across too many screens. Each screen was a chance to lose them.

I consolidated the flow by bringing ticket type and date selection into the event detail page itself, reducing the steps between "interested" and "paid" by two full screens. The checkout page was streamlined to show only what matters: what you're buying, how much, and how to pay.

Challenge 3

Event detail page: the information that drives decisions

The original event detail page tried to show everything at once: description, lineup, venue info, reviews, pricing, T&C, organizer details. Users were scrolling past the information they actually needed to make a buying decision.

I restructured the page around the four things that matter most: price and availability (visible above the fold), location with a map, social proof (attendee count, ratings), and a clear sticky CTA. Secondary information like full descriptions and T&C moved below the fold. The result was a page that answers "should I go?" in the first 3 seconds of scrolling.

Process

Running usability testing without a researcher

At Goers I didn't have a dedicated researcher, so I ran all usability testing myself. For each major redesign, I wrote test scripts, recruited 5-7 participants, facilitated moderated sessions, and synthesized findings into prioritized design changes. This was the first time the team had a structured testing practice, and it became part of the default workflow for new features by the time I left.

Impact

Three major redesigns in 7 months

Reflection

What I learned

Goers was a different pace from fintech. The product cycle was faster, the decisions were more gut-driven, and I had to wear both the designer and researcher hats. The biggest takeaway was that establishing a lightweight usability testing habit is more valuable than doing one big research project. Five quick tests beat one perfect study every time.
